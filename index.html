Policy & Infrastructure

The Energy Transition Challenge

Rapid scaling of E20 fuel cuts import bills but presents critical infrastructure and operational risks for public/heavy transit fleets.

  • Policy Objectives: National E20 biofuel integration supports carbon reduction but accelerates chemical wear on standard engine parts.
  • Depot Vulnerabilities: Large transit depots and fuel storages suffer phase separation and water accumulation in high relative humidity.
  • Operational Service Delays: Injector failures on public roads lead to transport delays, route downtime, and high repair deficits.
Operational Economics

Protecting Public Assets & Budgets

Preserving government transport utilities and heavy municipal transit assets under E20 dictates comparing three core routes:

  • Premium Fuel Pathway: Procuring premium-grade fuels to improve engine performance can increase fuel expenditure by 10–15%, making large-scale adoption financially challenging for public transport fleets.
  • Status Quo Pathway: Continued operation with conventional fuels alone may contribute to injector deposits, combustion-related carbon buildup, higher maintenance frequency, and reduced engine efficiency over time, particularly under heavy-duty operating conditions.
  • Sundar Carbon Pathway: A locally developed fuel conditioning technology designed to support cleaner combustion, protect critical fuel-system components, and improve operational efficiency—without requiring premium fuels or engine modifications.
The Threat

The Silent Engine Killer

Mandatory government ethanol blending (up to 20%) in modern petrol damages vital components.

  • Water Absorption: Ethanol attracts moisture from the air, creating a corrosive water-fuel mixture.
  • Corrosive Attacks: Rusts metal tanks and degrades rubber fuel lines.
  • BS4 & BS6 Danger: Highly sensitive high-pressure fuel injectors clog, causing engine misfires and loss of power.
